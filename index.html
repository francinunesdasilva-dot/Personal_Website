I grew up on a farm in the interior of Minas Gerais, Brazil — the kind of place where you learn early that nothing happens unless you move your hands. I left home young, worked, changed paths, learned English fast, and eventually found myself graduating from Harvard and building a life in the U.S.

These days I’m drawn to two things at once: the outer world (cities, trails, museums, airports, national parks), and the inner one (silence, attention, breath, the quiet work of becoming steadier). I paint to stay honest. I read to keep my mind awake. I go outside to remember what matters.

Who I am

Curiosity, discipline, and softness

My life has had a lot of sharp turns — and I’ve learned to trust them. I’m someone who likes building a life with intention, not just momentum. I’ve moved between worlds: rural Brazil and big American cities, survival and study, art and finance, quiet and intensity.

How I move through the world

I’m practical, but I’m not cynical. I like goals, but I also respect seasons. I can sprint when it’s time — and I can sit still when the best thing to do is stop trying to force an answer.

What keeps me grounded

Painting, nature, and meditation. Long walks. Good books. Random internet rabbit holes that start with one question and end three hours later with twenty tabs open and a new obsession.

Travel

The world, seen slowly

I’ve visited 12 countries and 34 U.S. National Parks. Travel, for me, isn’t about checking boxes — it’s about getting close to places. I love the feeling of arriving somewhere new and letting it teach me: different rhythms, different rules, different ways people solve the same human problems.

National Parks

Parks are where I remember how small and how lucky I am. I like early mornings, quiet trails, and the kind of landscapes that reset your nervous system without you noticing.

Cities & culture

I also love cities — the architecture, the noise, the food, the energy. I’m drawn to places with history you can feel in the street layout and the way people talk about home.

Paintings

Where I tell the truth without words

Painting is my way of staying in touch with what I actually feel. It slows me down. It makes me look longer. It’s also the one place where I don’t need to be “productive” — I just need to be present.

Reflections

Writing, when silence isn’t enough

I write the way I paint: to notice what’s true. Sometimes it’s clarity. Sometimes it’s contradiction. Usually it’s me trying to understand motion — the kind you can see in the sky, and the kind you feel inside.

While Everything Moves

Everything passes.
Look up at the sky and notice how the clouds keep moving…
After just a few seconds of watching them, they are no longer in the same place.

And inside—what is there?
Amid so many uncertainties, emptiness, and loneliness, everything is still in motion.
We may not yet know why, but movement is necessary.
Life is movement.

No matter how misaligned things may seem, they are, in truth, connected on an invisible plane.
Even if we don’t understand how, this movement was planned and is part of a greater journey;
a journey we are walking at every moment.

We don’t know where we are going, yet we continue forward, carried by movement itself.
What shapes our experience along the way are the choices we make.

The secret of life, then, lies in choosing what is right—
what makes sense—while we are in motion.

Tomorrow

I am running because I have a place to be 
A place that is magical and sweet
Where pain shall not exist
But colorful balloons, dance, and light

I am rushing to get to this place
As I need to live in it
If there is something so beautiful
How could one live a different life otherwise?

But there is a cost to get to this place
A price that few are willing to pay
And that is for not living in the present
And perhaps in constant pain

The place I long for is called tomorrow.
I will keep on chasing tomorrow
Even though I don't know
if it will exist

Meditation

Stillness as a skill

Meditation isn’t a “vibe” for me — it’s practice. Some days it feels like peace. Some days it feels like facing myself with no distractions. Over time it’s taught me something simple and hard: I don’t have to believe every thought, and I don’t have to run from every feeling.

How it shows up in my life

It helps me keep my attention clean. It makes the day feel less reactive. It’s the difference between “life happening to me” and “me being here for my life.”

8-day silent retreat

I did an 8-day silent retreat — no phone, no talking, no performing. Just routine, breath, and the quiet kind of work you can’t shortcut. It reset something in me.

The monastery — a place that made silence feel like a teacher.
